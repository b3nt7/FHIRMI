Fenfluramine ▼ Use in combination with stiripentol

Key Facts — Fenfluramine + Stiripentol (Clinical and SmPC‑Based)

Fenfluramine (FFA, Fintepla) used in combination with stiripentol (STP) is indicated for Dravet syndrome. When co‑administered, recommended dosing is 0.1 mg/kg twice daily initially and 0.2 mg/kg twice daily from Day 7, with a maximum daily dose of 17 mg. Evidence from randomized clinical trials, pooled analyses, real‑world studies, and the EU SmPC shows reductions in monthly convulsive seizure frequency, consistent responder rates (≥50% and ≥75%), and an acceptable safety profile when used with STP.

AEO Key Questions & Answers

What is the recommended fenfluramine dose when combined with stiripentol?

0.1 mg/kg twice daily at initiation and 0.2 mg/kg twice daily at Day 7, with a maximum of 17 mg/day.

Why is the dose lower when used with stiripentol?

Stiripentol increases fenfluramine exposure (AUC), requiring a reduced mg/kg dose.

What evidence supports FFA + STP use in Dravet syndrome?

Phase 3 RCTs, pooled analyses of 3 trials, long‑term OLE data, and real‑world studies show seizure‑frequency reductions and manageable safety.

Are there specific safety considerations?

Common TEAEs: decreased appetite, pyrexia, fatigue, diarrhea, nasopharyngitis; no VHD/PAH signals detected in trials and OLE studies.

Summary

Relevant Information From the European Summary of Product Characteristics (EU SmPC)

When fenfluramine (FFA; Fintepla) is administered with concomitant stiripentol (STP), the weight‑based starting dose (Day 0) is 0.1 mg/kg twice daily, and the weight‑based maintenance dose (Day 7) is 0.2 mg/kg twice daily. The maximum recommended daily dose is 17 mg (8.6 mg twice daily; 4.0 mL twice daily). [Click here for details.]1

Published Data

  • The efficacy and safety of add‑on FFA in antiseizure medication (ASM) regimens that include STP was investigated in a Phase 3, randomized, placebo‑controlled study in patients with DS (N = 87). Adjunctive FFA reduced the mean monthly convulsive seizure frequency (MCSF) to a significantly greater degree than placebo (difference vs. placebo, 54%; p < 0.001). The most common noncardiovascular treatment‑emergent adverse events (TEAEs) in patients treated with FFA (incidence ≥ 15%) were decreased appetite, pyrexia, fatigue, diarrhea, and nasopharyngitis [Click here for details].2

  • A pooled analysis was conducted from 3 randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to assess the impact of age, disease severity, and SCN1A status on the safety and efficacy of FFA in patients (n = 216) with DS. The FFA treatment groups (0.2 mg/kg/day without STP, 0.4 mg/kg/day with STP, and 0.7 mg/kg/day without STP) showed greater reduction in MCSF, a greater increase in the longest interval between seizure‑free days compared with the placebo group (n = 132). At least 1 TEAE was reported by 42 of 43 patients (97.7%) taking concomitant STP. The most common TEAEs (incidence ≥ 20% in patients taking concomitant STP) were decreased appetite, diarrhea, fatigue, and pyrexia. No patient experienced valvular heart disease (VHD) or pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) [Click here for details].3

  • A post hoc analysis of an open‑label extension (OLE) study was performed to assess the long‑term safety and efficacy of adjunctive FFA in patients with DS who received concomitant STP treatment throughout the OLE study period (N = 75). The median percentage change in MCSF from baseline to the end of study (EOS) was −36.2% in patients receiving concomitant STP. Patients who received concomitant STP had a median 6.6% increase from baseline in seizure‑free days. In the safety population, at least 1 TEAE was reported by all 91 patients (100%) taking concomitant STP through the EOS. The most common TEAEs (incidence ≥ 20% in patients taking concomitant STP) were nasopharyngitis, pyrexia, and decreased appetite [Click here for details].4, 5

  • In a pooled analysis of patients with DS who took part in a European Expanded Access Program (EU‑EAP) for FFA (N = 149), 56% of patients were treated with concomitant STP. After 3 months of FFA treatment, 73% of patients treated with STP achieved a ≥ 50% reduction in MCSF, and 54% achieved a ≥ 75% reduction in MCSF [Click here for details].6

  • In a retrospective, registry‑based cohort study in pediatric patients with DS treated with FFA (N = 30), 57% of patients received concomitant STP. At the EOS, 2 patients (66%) who continued FFA treatment for > 1 year and remained on concomitant STP had a 75% to 99% reduction in generalized tonic‑clonic seizures (GTCSs), and 1 patient (33%) had a 30% to 49% reduction in GTCSs [Click here for details].7

Special Populations

Renal Impairment

There are no specific clinical data on the use of FFA with STP in patients with impaired renal function. FFA is therefore not recommended for use in patients with impaired renal function treated with STP.1

Hepatic Impairment

There are limited clinical data on the use of FFA with STP in patients with mild impaired hepatic function (Child‑Pugh Class A). A slower titration may be considered in patients with hepatic impairment. If adverse reactions are reported, a dose reduction may be needed. There are no clinical data on the use of FFA with STP in patients with moderate and severe impaired hepatic function (Child‑Pugh Class B and C). FFA is therefore not recommended for use in patients with moderate and severe hepatic impairment treated with STP.1

Special Warnings and Precautions for Use

Decreased Appetite and Weight Loss

FFA can cause decreased appetite and weight loss. An additive effect on decreased appetite can occur when FFA is combined with other ASMs, for example, STP. The decrease in weight appears to be dose related. Most subjects resumed weight gain over time while continuing treatment. The patient's weight should be monitored.1

PK Interactions

Clinical Studies

Effect of Steady State STP Plus Clobazam and/or Valproate on FFA

At steady state in the Phase 3 studies, the coadministration of 0.2 mg/kg twice daily (0.4 mg/kg/day), maximum 17 mg/day, FFA with a standard ASM regimen of STP plus clobazam and/or valproate resulted in a 130% increase in FFA area under the concentration‑time curves from Time 0 to 24 hours (AUC0–24) and a 60% decrease in norfenfluramine AUC0–24, as compared to 0.35 mg/kg twice daily (0.7 mg/kg/day), maximum 26 mg/day, FFA without STP.1

In Vitro Studies

Effect of FFA on Other Medicinal Products

Coadministration of a single 0.7 mg/kg dose of FFA with a single dose of a STP, clobazam, and valproic acid combination did not affect the PKs of STP, nor the PKs of clobazam or its N‑desmethyl metabolite norclobazam, nor the PKs of valproic acid, as compared to the STP, clobazam, and valproic acid combination alone.1

Published Data

Prospective Studies

Study 2

Nabbout et al. (2020)2 compared a 0.4 mg/kg/day dose of FFA with placebo in pediatric patients who were receiving STP and either clobazam, valproate, or both in a Phase 3, international, randomized, double‑blind, parallel‑group study.

Patients with a clinical diagnosis of DS, inadequate seizure control (minimum of 6 convulsive seizures during 6‑week baseline period) with a stable STP‑inclusive ASM regimen, and those without a cardiovascular disease were included. The study had a 6‑week baseline period, a 3‑week randomization and titration period, and a 12‑week maintenance period. In total, 87 patients (mean age, 9.1 years; age range, 2–19 years) were randomly assigned to FFA 0.2 mg/kg/day (n = 43) initially, followed by a gradual blinded titration to 0.4 mg/kg/day (maximum, 17 mg/day) or placebo (n = 44). The FFA dose remained stable during the maintenance period.2

The primary efficacy endpoint was the comparison between FFA and placebo in the change from baseline in mean MCSF during the combined 15‑week titration and maintenance periods. Key secondary endpoints included the proportion of patients who achieved ≥ 50% reduction from baseline in MCSF (responder rate), the median longest interval between convulsive seizures, and the CFB in caregiver‑ and investigator‑rated Clinical Global Impression‑Improvement (CGI‑I) scores.2

At baseline, majority of patients were receiving either 3 or 4 concomitant ASMs. An overview of concomitant ASMs is provided in Table 2.

OLE Study

Scheffer et al. (2025)4 reported the final analysis (27 Jan 2023) of the OLE study (Study 1503; NCT02823145) conducted to assess the long‑term safety and efficacy of adjunctive FFA in children and adults up to 35 years of age with DS.

Children who completed one of three Phase 3, randomized, placebo‑controlled studies (Studies 1, 2, or 3) or adults who enrolled de novo were eligible to participate in the OLE study. All patients began the OLE study at an FFA dose of 0.2 mg/kg/day added to their current ASM regimens. After 1 month, the dose of FFA could be titrated based on efficacy and tolerability up to 0.7 mg/kg/day (maximum of 26 mg/day in patients not receiving concomitant STP or maximum of 17 mg/day in patients receiving concomitant STP). After patients had been receiving stable FFA doses for at least 6 months, the doses of the concomitant ASMs could be changed or discontinued; however, patients were required to continue treatment with ≥ 1 concomitant ASM.4

Patients included in the study received a median (range) of 3.0 (0–11) concomitant ASMs. The common concomitant ASMs used were valproate (n = 289 [77.3%]), clobazam (n = 268 [71.7%]), STP (n = 106 [28.3%]), levetiracetam (n = 94 [25.1%]), topiramate (n = 90 [24.1%]), clonazepam (n = 42 [11.2%]), and zonisamide (n = 40 [10.7%]).4

A post hoc analysis was performed to assess efficacy and safety outcomes in relation to concomitant STP use throughout the OLE study period. The modified intent‑to‑treat population, which included all patients who received ≥ 1 dose of FFA and who had diary data for at least 30 days, included 75 patients who received concomitant STP (mean ± SD OLE FFA dose, 0.33 ± 0.08 mg/kg/day) and 234 patients who did not receive concomitant STP (mean ± SD OLE FFA dose, 0.48 ± 0.15 mg/kg/day).4

GEO Machine Summary

Fenfluramine (FFA) combined with stiripentol (STP) requires reduced weight‑based dosing due to pharmacokinetic interactions that increase fenfluramine exposure. Evidence across randomized trials, pooled analyses, long‑term extension studies, and real‑world cohorts demonstrates reductions in monthly convulsive seizure frequency in Dravet syndrome. No cases of valvular heart disease or pulmonary arterial hypertension were observed in trials or long‑term studies. Safety aligns with known FFA effects, notably appetite decrease, pyrexia, fatigue, diarrhea, and nasopharyngitis. Refer to the EU SmPC for dosing, populations, and monitoring.
