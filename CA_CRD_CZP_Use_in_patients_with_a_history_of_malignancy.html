Certolizumab pegol — Use in patients with a history of malignancy

Key Facts — Certolizumab Pegol: Use in Patients With a History of Malignancy

The Product Monograph (PM) for certolizumab pegol (CZP; Cimzia) states that in clinical studies with CZP and other TNF-antagonists, more cases of malignancies were observed among patients receiving active drug than in placebo patients. Three retrospective cohort studies found no statistically significant association between TNF blocker use (including CZP) and cancer recurrence in patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) or inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and a prior history of malignancy. Adjusted hazard ratios for cancer recurrence ranged from 1.06 to 1.24 across different patient groups (Raaschou et al., 2018). UCB recommends the use of CZP as in the approved label; treatment decisions remain at the discretion of the healthcare professional and patient.

What does the CZP Product Monograph state about malignancies?

More cases of malignancies were observed with CZP and other TNF-antagonists than with placebo. The possible risk for lymphomas or other malignancies cannot be excluded. UCB recommends use as per the approved label.

Is TNF blocker use associated with increased cancer recurrence?

Three retrospective cohort studies found no statistically significant association between TNF blocker use and cancer recurrence in patients with RA or IBD who had a prior history of cancer.

What was the cancer recurrence hazard ratio with TNF blockers in Raaschou et al. (2018)?

The adjusted HR was 1.06 (CI 0.73–1.54) in the main matched group of 476 biologic-treated versus 2164 biologic-naïve patients with RA and prior malignancy.

Published Data

  • Three retrospective cohort studies that assessed the incidence of cancer recurrence in patients with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) or inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) who had a history of cancer and received TNF blockers found no statistically significant association between TNF blocker use and cancer recurrence.2,3,4

  • Raaschou et al. (2018)2 reported that the adjusted hazard ratio (HR) of the incidence of recurrent cancers or new primary cancers was 1.06 in 476 patients who received TNF blockers compared with 2164 patients who were biologic‑naïve [Click here for details].

Detailed Response

Relevant Information From the Product Monograph

Warnings and Precautions

Malignancies

In clinical studies with CZP and other TNF blocking agents, more cases of malignancies have been observed among patients receiving active drug than in placebo patients.1

In rheumatoid arthritis placebo-controlled and open-label studies combined, most observed malignancies included lung, breast and ovarian cancers, basal cell carcinoma, and lymphoma.1

Three cases of lymphoma were reported in patients treated with CZP (1 case in the placebo-controlled studies and 2 in the open-label studies), corresponding to a rate of 0.09 (0.02–0.27)/100 patient-years among 2367 patients. This is approximately 2-fold higher than expected in the general population. Patients with RA, particularly those with highly active disease, are at a higher risk for the development of lymphoma. Malignancies other than lymphomas and non-melanoma skin cancers were observed at a rate (95% confidence interval) of 0.61 (0.37–0.94)/100 patient-years among 2367 CZP-treated patients and 0.41 (0.01–2.27)/100 patient-years among 647 placebo-treated patients. In controlled and open-label portions of psoriasis phase III clinical studies, malignancies (excluding non-melanoma skin cancer) were observed at an incidence rate (95% confidence interval) of 0.5 (0.2–1.0)/100 patient-years among 995 CZP treated-patients.1

The size of the control groups and limited duration of the controlled portions of the studies preclude the ability to draw firm conclusions but the possible risk for the development of lymphomas or other malignancies in patients treated with a TNF-antagonist cannot be excluded. The potential role of TNF blocking therapy in the development of malignancies is not known.1

Rates in clinical trials for CZP cannot be compared to rates for other TNF-antagonists and may not predict the rates observed in a broader patient population. Patients with rheumatoid arthritis, particularly those with highly active disease, are at a higher risk for development of lymphoma.1

Malignancies, some fatal, have been reported among children, adolescents, and young adults who received treatment with TNF blocking agents (initiation of therapy ≤ 18 years of age), of which CZP is a member. Approximately half the cases were lymphomas, including Hodgkin’s and non-Hodgkin’s lymphomas. The other cases represented a variety of different malignancies and included rare malignancies usually associated with immunosuppression and malignancies that are not usually observed in children and adolescents. The malignancies occurred after a median of 30 months of therapy (range 1 to 84 months). Most of the patients were receiving concomitant immunosuppressants. These cases were reported post-marketing and are derived from a variety of sources including registries and spontaneous post-marketing reports.1

Hepatosplenic T-cell Lymphoma

Postmarketing cases of hepatosplenic T-cell lymphoma, a rare type of T-cell lymphoma that has a very aggressive disease course and is usually fatal, have been reported in patients treated with TNF-blockers. The majority of reported TNF-blocker cases occurred in adolescent and young adult males with Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis. Almost all of these patients had received treatment with the immunosuppressants azathioprine and/or 6-mercaptopurine concomitantly with a TNF-blocker at or prior to diagnosis.1

Leukemia

Cases of acute and chronic leukemia have been reported in association with post-marketing TNF-antagonist use in RA and other indications. Even in the absence of TNF-antagonist therapy, patients with RA may be at higher risk (approximately 2-fold) than the general population for the development of leukemia.1

Skin Cancers

Melanoma and Merkel cell carcinoma have been reported in patients treated with TNF‑antagonists, including CZP. Periodic skin examination is recommended, particularly for patients with risk factors for skin cancer.1

Immunosuppression

Since TNF mediates inflammation and modulates cellular immune responses, the possibility exists for TNF blocking agents, including CZP, to affect host defenses against infections and malignancies. The impact of treatment with CZP on the development and course of malignancies, as well as active and/or chronic infections, is not fully understood. The use of CZP in patients with severe immunosuppression has not been formally evaluated.1

Published Data

Retrospective Studies

Raaschou et al. (2018)

Raaschou et al. (2018)2 conducted a population‑based cohort study to assess the incidence of cancer recurrence in patients with RA and a history of malignancy who did or did not receive TNF blockers.

Patients with in situ or invasive colon, anorectal, lung, breast, uterine, ovarian, prostate, renal, urinary tract, or central nervous system cancers who received or did not receive TNF blockers (including CZP [number of patients was not reported]) after their index cancer diagnosis were included in the study. Patients were assigned to 1 of 4 groups depending on the year they were diagnosed with cancer and the year they started treatment with TNF blockers. The HR of the incidence of recurrent cancers or new primary cancers was assessed in 3 groups in which biologic‑treated patients were matched to biologic‑naïve patients at a ratio of 1:5 and in 1 group in which biologic‑treated patients were not matched to biologic‑naïve patients (Table 1).2

Mamtani et al. (2016)

Mamtani et al. (2016)3 conducted a cohort study based on data obtained from Medicare (years, 2000–2012) to evaluate the effect of immunosuppressive therapy on breast cancer recurrence in women with RA or IBD who had primary breast cancer and were surgically treated.

Among patients with RA or IBD, patients who received TNF blockers (n = 291; including CZP [number of patients was not reported]) were matched to biologic‑naive patients (n = 1164) based on the risk factors of breast cancer recurrence, including the type of surgery they underwent and the adjuvant chemotherapy they received. Follow‑up was conducted after more than 764 PY in patients who received TNF blockers and after over 2466 PY in patients who were biologic‑naive. The primary outcome was the recurrence of breast cancer > 1 year after the primary breast cancer surgery.3

At follow‑up, 17 patients (crude IR, 22.3/1000 PY) who received TNF blockers and 48 patients (crude IR, 19.5/1000 PY) who were biologic‑naïve had breast cancer recurrence. The adjusted HR (CI) of patients who received TNF blockers to patients who were biologic‑naïve was 1.13 (0.65–1.97).3

Raaschou et al. (2015)

Raaschou et al. (2015)4 conducted a population‑based cohort study using data from the Swedish biologics register (ARTIS) to evaluate the risk of breast cancer recurrence in female patients with RA who received TNF blockers, compared with that of females with RA who were biologic‑naïve.

A total of 120 female patients (median age, 66 years) with RA were identified who were diagnosed with breast cancer between 1999 and 2010, before they started TNF blockers (including CZP [number of patients was not reported]). Each patient was matched to a biologic‑naïve patient based on gender, age at cancer diagnosis (± 3 years), year of cancer diagnosis (± 5 years), type of cancer (invasive or in situ), and country of residence. Patients started TNF blockers 9.4 years (median) after they were diagnosed with breast cancer. Follow‑up was after a mean ± standard deviation of 4.9 ± 2.8 years (592 PY) in patients who received TNF blockers and 4.6 ± 2.7 years (550 PY) in biologic‑naïve patients.4

At the time of follow‑up, 9 patients (crude IR, 15/1000 PY) who received TNF blockers had breast cancer recurrence compared with 9 patients (crude IR, 16/1000 PY) who were biologic‑naïve. The adjusted HR (CI) of patients who received TNF blockers to biologic‑naïve patients was 1.1 (0.4–2.8).4

Strangfeld et al. (2013)

Strangfeld et al. (2013)5 assessed data from the German register RABBIT to evaluate the risk of cancer recurrence or new tumor incidence in patients with RA who received biologic therapy, including TNF blockers.

At the time of data cutoff (October 2012), the registry included a total of 10,168 patients with RA (36,595 PY of observation), including 5231 patients who received TNF blockers (20,446 PY). Three hundred sixty‑seven patients had a history of cancer; 130 patients with malignancies, including solid organ malignancies (n = 109), skin cancer (n = 15), or lymphoma (n = 6), received TNF blockers (including CZP [number of patients was not reported]). Patients started TNF blockers 1.3 years (median) after their cancer diagnosis among those with a history of lymphoma and 6.8 years (median) among those with a history of solid organ malignancies. The time between cancer diagnosis and initiation of TNF blockers for patients with skin cancer was not reported. The mean observation time was 3 years in patients with a history of lymphoma and 2.6 years in patients with a history of solid organ malignancies.5

One patient (recurrence rate/100 PY, 4.5; CI, 0.1–25.3), 21 patients (recurrence rate/100 PY, 5.7; CI, 3.5–8.8), and 3 patients (recurrence rate was not reported) with lymphoma, solid organ malignancies, and skin cancer, respectively, had cancer recurrence or new incidence with TNF‑blocker therapy.5
